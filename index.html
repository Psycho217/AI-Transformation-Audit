The Thesis

Why this works

Businesses don't buy AI. They buy higher revenue, lower costs, and faster execution. This framework connects technology to those outcomes.

The market is flooded with "AI consultants" who pitch chatbots. That's a solution looking for a problem. The approach here inverts it: start with the business, find the problems, then prescribe the right tool — which may or may not be AI.

The deliverable isn't a technology demo. It's a consulting-grade report that speaks the language of business owners: margins, break-even timelines, and payback periods.

The shift in framing: Instead of "We'll build you an AI chatbot," you say "We'll reduce your support costs by 60% in 3 months, with a 4-month payback on your investment." Same technology. Completely different conversation.

Phase 1

Understand the business

Before you can improve anything, you need to understand how the company makes money, spends money, and operates day-to-day. Not how they think they operate — how they actually operate.

How to do this

Schedule 2–3 sessions with the business owner and key team members. Don't come with a pitch deck. Come with questions. Record everything (with permission). Your job is to listen and map the territory.

Revenue
  • How they make money
  • Pricing model
  • Revenue streams
  • Payment channels
Customers
  • Who buys
  • Why they buy
  • How they find the business
  • What makes them leave
Operations
  • Daily workflow
  • Team structure
  • Tools in use
  • Manual vs automated
Finance
  • Cost structure
  • Margins
  • Cash flow patterns
  • Growth constraints

The discovery framework

Cover these five areas in order. Each one builds context for the next.

1

Revenue model

How does money come in? What's the split between channels? Is revenue recurring, transactional, or project-based? What's the average deal size?

2

Customer journey

How do customers find the business? What's the sales cycle? Where do prospects drop off? What triggers repeat purchases? What makes customers leave?

3

Operations & team

Walk through a typical day. Who does what? What tools do they use? Where do things break or slow down? What's the communication flow between teams?

4

Cost structure

What are the biggest expenses? Fixed vs variable costs. Where is money being spent that doesn't directly generate revenue? What would they cut if they had to?

5

Growth blockers

What's stopping them from doubling revenue? What would they automate if they could? What keeps the founder up at night? Where do they spend time they shouldn't?

Practical tip: Most business owners will tell you what they think is important. Your job is to ask follow-up questions until you find what's actually happening. "Walk me through what happened the last time that went wrong" is worth more than any questionnaire.

Phase 2

Map every process

Turn what you learned into visual flowcharts. Every business runs on processes — most of them undocumented, inconsistent, and full of hidden manual steps.

How to do this

For each core operation, create a step-by-step flowchart. Start with the trigger (what kicks off the process) and end with the outcome. Include every handoff, decision point, and waiting period.

Phase 3

Measure what matters

Numbers are the baseline. Without them, you can't prove that anything you recommend actually helped. Measure before you change.

How to do this

For each process you mapped, attach concrete numbers. Some you'll get from their accounting software. Others you'll need to estimate by observing or asking: "How long does this take? How often does it happen? How many people are involved?"

The metrics that matter

Revenue health

  • Monthly recurring revenue — top-line trend
  • Gross profit margin — efficiency of delivery
  • Net profit margin — what's actually left
  • Average order value — revenue per transaction
  • Customer lifetime value — long-term worth
  • Customer acquisition cost — price of growth
  • Churn rate — the leak in the bucket

Operational efficiency

  • Time per task — minutes spent on each step
  • People per process — headcount involved
  • Cost per operation — rupees spent per unit
  • Error rate — how often things go wrong
  • Customer wait time — from request to delivery
  • Throughput — volume handled per day
  • Rework rate — what gets done twice

The key ratio: LTV to CAC. If lifetime value is less than 3x acquisition cost, the business has a structural problem that no amount of AI will fix. Flag it early.

Phase 4

Identify AI opportunities

Now you know how the business works, where it's slow, and what the numbers say. The question isn't "where can we use AI?" It's "which problems are best solved by AI vs simpler automation vs process change?"

How to think about this

For each bottleneck or manual step you identified, run it through these six questions. If the answer to any of them is yes, there's an opportunity.

Matching problems to solutions

Not everything needs a large language model. Here's how different problems map to different tools:

Business problemRight toolHow it works
Processing invoices, receipts, forms OCR + LLM extraction Scan document, extract structured data, validate against rules, push to accounting system.
Answering customer questions RAG chatbot + escalation Index company docs. AI answers from knowledge base. Escalates to human when confidence is low.
Qualifying sales leads Scoring model Train on conversion data. Score leads by likelihood to close. Route hot leads immediately.
Writing emails and follow-ups LLM assistant Draft replies based on context. Personalize outreach. Extract action items automatically.
Generating weekly reports Automated dashboards Pull data, compute KPIs, generate visual report, email to stakeholders. Zero manual effort.
Analyzing sales calls Speech-to-text + LLM Transcribe, extract key points, identify objections, score rep performance.
Finding information internally RAG over internal docs Index SOPs and policies. Team asks questions in natural language instead of searching folders.
Predicting demand or churn ML forecasting Historical patterns to future predictions. Optimize inventory, staffing, or outreach proactively.

Important distinction: Not every opportunity requires AI. "Employee copies data from email to spreadsheet" is solved by a simple Zapier integration, not a language model. Use the simplest tool that works.

Phase 5

Calculate ROI

Every recommendation gets a number. If you can't quantify it, don't recommend it. This is what separates a professional audit from a blog post.

How to do this

For each opportunity, calculate three things: what it costs today, what it will cost after implementation, and what the implementation itself costs.

How to read this

Do first

Quick wins. They build trust, generate immediate savings, and fund the bigger projects. Start here always.

Plan next

Worth doing, but need planning, budget, and time. Start these once quick wins prove value and pay for themselves.

Nice to have

Do them if there's spare capacity. Don't let them distract from higher-value work.

Skip for now

Look impressive but don't move the needle enough to justify the investment today.

Phase-by-phase detail

Month 1 — Quick wins

Foundation and immediate value

Set up CRM automation (lead assignment, follow-up triggers, task creation). Deploy AI email assistant for draft replies and action extraction. Build automated KPI dashboard pulling from existing data sources.

Expected result: 20–30 hours/month recovered. ₹30K–50K/month in savings.

Month 2 — Core systems

Data processing and intelligence

Build document processing pipeline (invoices, contracts, forms → structured data → accounting system). Train and deploy lead scoring model on historical conversion data.

Expected result: Invoice processing time drops 80%. Lead conversion improves 15–25%.

Month 3 — AI agents

Intelligent automation

Deploy customer support AI agent with RAG over company knowledge base. Multi-channel (web chat, WhatsApp, email). Human escalation for complex issues.

Expected result: 60–70% of support queries handled automatically. Response time drops from hours to seconds.

Month 4+ — Advanced

Predictive and strategic

ML forecasting for demand, revenue, and churn. Executive dashboard with natural language queries. Continuous optimization loop.

Expected result: Proactive decisions instead of reactive. Cumulative savings exceed ₹1.5L/month.

Handling the data access challenge

The biggest practical hurdle: companies won't share detailed financials upfront. The solution is progressive trust-building.

  1. Start with public data. Analyze what's visible — website, reviews, job postings, industry benchmarks.
  2. Deliver something valuable for free. "Here's what I noticed about your customer flow just from your website" opens doors.
  3. Ask for limited access first. One process, one department, one metric. Prove value in a small scope.
  4. Sign NDA, then go deep. Once they trust you, formal agreement + full access for the complete audit.

Your edge: With a background in backend engineering, AI agents, RAG systems, and enterprise workflows — you're not just recommending, you're building. The audit creates demand. The implementation fulfills it. That's a complete business, not just a one-time deliverable.
