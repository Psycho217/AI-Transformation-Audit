Survival business vs. growth business

A business is either in survival mode (all energy goes to paying monthly expenses) or growth mode (survival happens by default, energy goes to scale). Ziiroo must build as a growth business from day one.

Survival businessGrowth business (what we build)
Focused only on monthly survival — paying rent, bills, suppliersFocused on scale and expansion — survival is a byproduct
Great at product/service, but poor at marketing & salesBalance: great product/service AND great income-generating skills
Reactive marketing — word-of-mouth only, inconsistent income80% marketing (value addition), 20% selling (promotion)
Owner-driven — one person does everything, bottleneckFunction-driven — systems and teams run each function

Critical rule — the 80/20 of marketing vs. sales: 80% of effort goes to value addition (education, content, building credibility). 20% goes to promotion (pitching, selling). Only sales fails. Without value addition first, even good products get ignored.

The 80/20 marketing principle — value first, sell second

Only sales fails. Directly pitching without adding value = ignored, blocked, muted. The right balance:

Wrong: Only selling

"Hi, I'm from Ziiroo, we do BI + AI consulting, do you need our services?"

→ Ignored. No credibility. No value. No relationship built.

→

Right: 80% value, 20% sell

8 weeks of posts showing how you audit processes, save money, build AI systems — real insights, real numbers.

→ Week 9: "Want us to do this for your business?" People listen because you've proven expertise.

List your customers' fears, problems, pain points, needs, desires. Each one = a content topic. Give solutions that showcase expertise. That IS marketing.

Function 3

Operations

Deliver every engagement on time, on quality, and on budget. Operations is how we fulfill what sales promised.

Pain Without structured operations

  • Scope creep — no clear boundaries, projects expand without additional revenue
  • Missed deadlines — no project tracking, things slip without visibility
  • Inconsistent quality — output varies depending on mood, not process
  • No templates/SOPs — rebuilding from scratch each engagement
  • Communication gaps — client doesn't know status, has to ask
  • No capacity planning — overcommit, underdeliver, burn out

The failure chain — why inconsistency in ONE function kills all three

If this breaksWhat happens downstream
Marketing inconsistentNo leads → Sales has nobody to sell to → Pipeline dries up → Revenue drops → Operations has no work
Sales inconsistentLeads come in but don't convert → Operations has no projects → Revenue stalls → Marketing spend feels wasted
Operations inconsistentDelivery is poor → No testimonials, no case studies → Marketing has no proof → Trust breaks → No referrals, no repeat business

This is why we build all three simultaneously, not sequentially. They're interconnected. A broken link anywhere collapses the chain. Consistency in all three is the minimum bar for a growth business.

When it's time to hire — the ASK hiring framework

From Marketing, Sales, Operations: pick ONE you're best at (likely Operations). Hire talented people for the other two. Use this system:

  1. List tasks & activities — write every specific task you expect this person to do. Not a ChatGPT job description. Actual tasks: "Make 15 cold calls/day," "Write 3 LinkedIn posts/week."
  2. For each task, define ASK — Attitude required (persistence, confidence), Skill required (writing, phone etiquette, data analysis), Knowledge required (industry, tools, processes).
  3. Prepare assessments — For attitude: psychometric assessment. For skill: give them the actual task as an assignment in the interview. For knowledge: test paper on domain fundamentals.
  4. Hire talent, not cheap labor — One talented person at premium salary > five people at ₹30K who need you to do the work. The wrong hire costs 3-5x their salary in wasted time.

Total setup cost: ₹0. Every tool mentioned has a free tier. The investment is time and focus, not money. Start with process. Add paid tools only when volume demands it.

Founder's Role

Your role as founder — management, not firefighting

Doing everything yourself is not commitment — it's a trap. Your job is management: strategy, functions, and people.

The 3 responsibilities of the founder

1. Strategy management

Create strategies for scale, improving profit margins, and improving cash flows. This is your #1 priority — nobody else will do it.

2. Functional management

Build systems and teams in each function. Then review them. Two functions at a time. Over 3 years, all 7 functions become self-running.

3. People management

Give feedback, hold people accountable, fire the wrong people, reward the right ones. Build a hiring system: list tasks → define attitude/skill/knowledge → prepare assessments.

The self-employment trap: You start a business, hire people, pay salaries — but end up doing the work your team should do. You become the bottleneck. Revenue stalls. The cure: management + one function you're best at. Hire for the other two. One person at a time, one department at a time.

ChangeWhat it means for ZiirooWhen

3. Enhance courage Courage to give fairly (pay for talent). Courage to make decisions (don't wait for perfect conditions). Courage to invest the money you don't have to make the money you desire. Always

Invest in 4 areas: Marketing (increase reach), Hiring (delegate to talented people), Automating (become efficient), Learning (make better decisions). These are investments, not expenses. Cutting them paralyzes growth.
